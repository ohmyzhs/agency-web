<h1>When 40% of Enterprise Apps Have AI Agents: Three Barriers Behind Gartner’s Forecast</h1>
<p class="zhs-html-lead">In August 2025, Gartner sent a strong signal to the IT industry. It forecast that by the end of 2026, 40% of all enterprise applications would include task-specific AI agents, a sharp increase from less than 5% in 2025. As of July 2026, that forecast is moving closer to reality. But the path is also exposing barriers that were not obvious at the starting line. The gap between technical possibility and organizational reality remains wide.</p>
</section>
<div class="zhs-html-divider"></div>

<h2>The forecast is becoming real</h2>
<p>Roughly a year after Gartner published its 40% forecast, the market is confirming its direction. Salesforce has integrated AI agents into CRM through Agentforce, ServiceNow has embedded AI agents into ITSM workflows, and SAP has begun supporting AI agents in ERP environments through Joule. With the general availability of AWS WorkSpaces for AI Agents, even legacy systems are becoming targets for AI automation, sharply expanding the scope of where agents can be applied.</p>
<p>The important point is that AI agent adoption is not just becoming a matter of “adding features.” It is becoming a business process redesign issue. Early deployments began with the automation of simple repetitive work, but in the first half of 2026, enterprises began integrating AI agents into core business processes. Agents are increasingly entering real workflows such as order processing, customer support, internal approvals, and data analysis report generation.</p>
<p>Still, several barriers remain before Gartner’s forecast can be fully realized. According to AgentMarketCap’s analysis, about 40% of companies that adopted AI agents said they needed major adjustments within six months of initial deployment. In other words, two different 40% figures are appearing at the same time: the 40% adoption forecast and the 40% adjustment requirement.</p>

<h2>Barrier 1: Integration with legacy systems</h2>
<p>The most practical barrier is integration with existing systems. Global technology companies often have modern API-based SaaS ecosystems, which makes AI agent adoption relatively straightforward. But many Korean companies — especially in finance, large-scale manufacturing, and the public sector — still operate decades-old legacy systems, including mainframes, older ERP systems, and internal tools built around ActiveX-era environments. In these settings, the APIs that AI agents need often do not exist at all.</p>
<p>AWS WorkSpaces for AI Agents, covered in the July 8 ZHS Daily Issue, has emerged as one answer to this problem, but it does not solve everything. Desktop UI operation is slower than API-based automation, and when the screen layout changes, the agent’s work logic must be updated as well. The fundamental solution is modernization of the legacy system itself, but that requires significant time and cost. Companies considering AI agent adoption have to decide where they stand between a temporary workaround and a structural fix.</p>

<h2>Barrier 2: Organizational governance and trust</h2>
<p>As AI agents begin to intervene in real business processes, a more fundamental question emerges: who is accountable for an agent’s decisions? As Anthropic’s “AI agent hiring guide” pointed out, the success or failure of agents depends less on the technology itself and more on organizational fundamentals: information transparency, role clarity, a north star or shared goal, and gradual trust-building.</p>
<p>For Korean companies, cultural characteristics around decision-making authority and accountability create additional considerations for AI agent adoption. Only organizations that can clearly answer the question, “If we follow the agent’s recommendation and something goes wrong, who is responsible?” can successfully deploy AI agents. This is not a pure technology problem. It is a problem of organization design and governance.</p>
<p>Samsung Electronics’ AX transformation case is instructive here. Alongside AI agent adoption, Samsung redesigned its organizational structure and decision-making processes. It clearly separated the areas where AI agents could make decisions from the areas where humans had to give final approval, and disclosed those criteria transparently across the company. This approach offers a practical answer to a trust problem that cannot be solved by technology adoption alone.</p>

<h2>Barrier 3: Cost efficiency and ROI proof</h2>
<p>The third barrier to AI agent adoption is cost. KAIST research released on July 5 quantified that the cost of a single agent run — including token costs, GPU compute costs, and electricity costs — is much higher than a simple chatbot conversation. That makes the CFO’s question sharper: “How much is this agent actually contributing to our company?”</p>
<p>The cost of adopting AI agents has fallen by 20–40% compared with 2025, but clear ROI measurement standards are still missing. Some consulting reports claim productivity improvements of 200% after AI agent adoption, but those figures are limited to specific tasks and are difficult to generalize across an entire enterprise. According to analysis by Korea IDC and bizpmlab, Korean companies typically need six to twelve months to confirm actual ROI from AI agent adoption, and uncertainty during that period remains one of the main reasons companies hesitate.</p>
<p>The lesson from ZHS’s own experience is straightforward: start small and begin with measurable areas. Trying to replace an entire business process with agents at once has a high probability of failure. A more effective approach is to start with unit tasks where clear KPIs — processing time, error rate, and cost — can be measured, then expand gradually.</p>

<h2>The reality and outlook for Korean companies</h2>
<p>Major Korean companies such as Samsung Electronics, Hyundai Motor, SK, and LG are actively adopting AI agents. Samsung Electronics has created a dedicated AX, or AI Transformation, organization and is gradually introducing AI agents across its DX division. Hyundai Motor is applying AI agents to manufacturing processes and logistics management. SK Group has established an AI task force under the SUPEX Council to coordinate AI agent adoption across group companies.</p>
<p>The situation is different for mid-sized companies and SMEs. Infrastructure costs, a shortage of specialized talent, and the absence of clear ROI remain obstacles. In particular, the IT infrastructure of Korean SMEs is often less modernized than that of large enterprises, which raises the entry barrier for AI agent adoption.</p>
<p>In the second half of 2026, several changes may help narrow this gap. First, as managed services such as AWS WorkSpaces for AI Agents become more common, the infrastructure cost barrier should decline. Second, as the open-source AI agent ecosystem, including Hermes Agent and OpenClaw, matures, SMEs will have more room to experiment without a heavy upfront burden. Third, as technologies that improve the power efficiency of AI agents — such as inference caching and lightweight model routing — continue to advance, operating costs should keep falling.</p>

<h2>The ZHS angle — what operating agents directly has revealed</h2>
<p>Since May 2026, ZHS has operated Hermes Agent and directly confirmed the value of a “start small, expand gradually” approach. The deployment began with simple content generation automation. Today, ZHS runs compound agent workflows connected to more than 34 tools. Hermes Agent handles a range of work, including SEO report generation, content distribution, social media management, and site monitoring.</p>
<p>The most important lesson from ZHS’s experience is that agents do not solve an organization’s problems. They reveal those problems faster and more clearly. If information is not organized, the agent produces poor results. If decision criteria are unclear, there is no reliable way to evaluate the agent’s output. The first step in agent adoption is not the technology itself. It is the cleanup of the organization’s information flows and decision-making processes. This matches exactly with the organizational fundamentals emphasized by Anthropic.</p>

<section class="zhs-html-conclusion">
<p>Gartner’s 40% forecast is becoming real. But for that “40%” to mean more than a headline number, companies have to overcome three barriers: legacy integration, organizational governance, and cost efficiency. The technology is already available. The question is whether organizations are ready to absorb it.</p>
<p>Based on its own Hermes Agent operations and AX cases from Samsung and Hyundai Motor, ZHS plans to prepare a practical checklist for Korean companies considering AI agent adoption. The success or failure of agent adoption depends less on the technology than on the organization adopting it.</p>
